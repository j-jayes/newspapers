# **Architecting High-Fidelity Extraction Pipelines for Mixed-Typography Historical Swedish Economic Data**

The digitization and structured extraction of economic data from highly degraded historical documents represents a profound challenge at the intersection of computational digital humanities, computer vision, and natural language processing. When addressing the specific domain of 19th- and early 20th-century Swedish newspapers—specifically those published between 1880 and 1926—the complexity of the task is magnified by multiple compounding factors. These source materials are characterized by severe material degradation, intricate and tightly packed multi-column layouts, dense tabular financial data, and, critically, the contemporaneous transition from Gothic (Fraktur) to Latin (Antiqua) typography. As archivists and data scientists seek to convert these raw pixel representations into highly faithful, machine-readable text that can be seamlessly processed by semantic extraction frameworks, the selection of the underlying optical character recognition (OCR) architecture becomes the foundational determinant of the entire project's success.

The integration of advanced semantic extraction tools, specifically Google's langextract library, imposes uncompromising, absolute requirements on the upstream optical character recognition layer. Because the langextract framework operates by mapping its structured entity outputs directly back to specific, exact character offsets within the source text to provide verifiable, visually grounded provenance, the input text must possess near-perfect character-level fidelity. Any hallucinated text, silent linguistic auto-corrections, or dropped characters fundamentally corrupt the offset indices. Once these indices are corrupted, the extraction becomes ungrounded, the interactive HTML visualizations fail to align with the source, and the integrity of the entire data pipeline is invalidated. Consequently, the selection of the transcription architecture—whether relying on a finely tuned traditional hybrid OCR pipeline or leveraging a frontier Vision Large Language Model (VLM)—must be dictated by rigorous empirical trade-offs concerning character error rates, systemic biases, temporal hallucination triggers, and operational latency at scale.

This comprehensive report conducts an exhaustive analysis of the current state-of-the-art in historical document transcription. By synthesizing recent empirical findings regarding multimodal large language models—specifically their performance on highly degraded 18th-century Russian texts and 19th-century English newspapers—alongside established benchmarks for Swedish historical OCR, this document provides a definitive architectural comparison. It delineates the specific failure modes and systematic biases inherent in modern Vision LLMs when confronted with non-standard typography, evaluates their handling of mixed Fraktur and Antiqua scripts, and ultimately delivers a highly specific, engineered pipeline recommendation designed to satisfy the rigorous grounding constraints of the langextract framework.

## **Architectural Comparison: Custom Hybrid Pipelines vs. Vision LLMs**

The current state-of-the-art for transcribing complex historical documents is defined by an ongoing architectural divergence between two distinct paradigms. The first is the highly specialized, modular hybrid pipeline, which concatenates layout analysis, line segmentation, character recognition, and linguistic post-correction into a sequential, deterministic workflow. The second is the holistic, end-to-end Vision Language Model paradigm, which processes the document image globally, relying on deep self-attention mechanisms to simultaneously infer spatial layout, semantic context, and character-level data in a single generative pass.

### **The Deterministic Modularity of Traditional Hybrid OCR Pipelines**

The traditional approach to historical document digitization relies on specialized open-source frameworks such as Tesseract, Kraken, and Calamari. These systems have evolved significantly from their origins as rule-based template matchers; modern iterations leverage deep Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) paired with bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks to achieve high accuracy on a variety of text types.1 In a standard hybrid pipeline, a dedicated layout analysis model first identifies text blocks, column boundaries, and reading order. A subsequent segmenter isolates individual text lines, which are then passed to the CNN-LSTM acoustic model to predict the final character sequence.

Empirical evaluations on Swedish historical newspapers highlight both the technical capabilities and the inherent, structural limitations of these modular systems. When processing subsets of the Kubhist corpus—a massive diachronic collection of Swedish historical newspaper texts spanning centuries—standard off-the-shelf OCR engines display significant variance in performance. Tesseract version 5.5.0, utilizing its modernized LSTM architecture, achieves a baseline Character Error Rate (CER) of approximately 6.7% and a Word Error Rate (WER) of 22.4% on 19th- and 20th-century Swedish data.3 Kraken, utilizing a pre-trained model specifically optimized for German historical materials (which share the dense Fraktur typography prevalent in Swedish texts of the era), yields a CER of 7.6% and a WER of 26.5%.3 The relatively high error rates observed in these zero-shot applications are partially attributable to specific linguistic mismatches. For instance, models trained exclusively on German historical corpora consistently fail to recognize specific Swedish graphemes, such as the letter "å", leading to systematic character drops and substitutions across the dataset.3

To overcome these baseline limitations and achieve acceptable fidelity, hybrid pipelines rely heavily on domain-specific fine-tuning, synthetic data generation, and cross-fold training regimens. Research utilizing the open-source Calamari engine on Swedish historical newspapers spanning the early 19th century demonstrates that mixed deep CNN-LSTM hybrid models, when trained using a combination of cross-fold validation and confidence voting mechanisms, can achieve a Character Accuracy Rate of 97.43%, corresponding to a highly optimized CER of 2.57%.4 Achieving this level of precision, however, requires the meticulous curation of ground-truth datasets that accurately represent the specific typographical idiosyncrasies of the target era.

The mixed script typography of the 1880–1926 Swedish era introduces severe operational bottlenecks for traditional pipelines. During this transitional period, it was highly common for newspapers to utilize angular Fraktur scripts for domestic reporting and general narrative text, while simultaneously employing rounded Antiqua scripts for foreign loan words, scientific terminology, financial tickers, and specialized economic data.3 The co-occurrence of these two highly distinct typefaces on the same page—and occasionally interspersed within the exact same sentence—confounds optical models trained exclusively on a single typeface. Historical analyses of Finnish and Swedish digitization efforts indicate that while OCR accuracy for Antiqua text is generally quite high, Fraktur presents persistent, highly complex challenges due to its dense, angular, and highly compressed visual characteristics, as well as its reliance on complex historical ligatures.3 To manage this typographical heterogeneity, modular pipelines must either utilize a highly robust, generalized mixed-model trained on perfectly balanced datasets of both scripts, or they must implement a computationally expensive upstream classification layer designed to route specific bounding boxes to dedicated, typeface-specific Fraktur or Antiqua decoders.4

The final, often necessary component of the traditional historical pipeline is post-OCR linguistic correction. This step treats the erroneous, raw OCR output as a noisy "source language" and attempts to translate it into a clean, orthographically correct "target language" using sequence-to-sequence transformer models. Applying the pre-trained ByT5 transformer model to Swedish 19th-century newspaper OCR outputs has been shown to reduce the overall CER by an impressive 36%.8 Systems like DocTR have proven highly compatible with this cross-model post-OCR correction approach, occasionally achieving lower final error rates than highly tuned Tesseract deployments.3

However, this sequential, multi-stage architecture is highly vulnerable to the phenomenon of "cascading errors." If the initial heuristic layout parser fails to accurately define precise bounding boxes around a dense, multi-column economic table, or if a line segmenter slightly miscalculates and slices through the tall ascenders or deep descenders of a Fraktur script, the downstream CNN-LSTM receives degraded, localized input. The optical model subsequently generates semantic gibberish that the final post-correction language model will completely fail to reconstruct, ultimately outputting hallucinated text that breaks any attempt at structured data extraction.9

### **The Paradigm Shift: Holistic Processing via Vision LLMs**

The rapid emergence of multimodal Vision Large Language Models (VLMs) represents a fundamental paradigm shift away from modular sequence-to-sequence prediction and toward holistic spatial-semantic reasoning. Vision LLMs natively ingest entire document images or large high-resolution crops and generate structured text in a single, unified pass, entirely bypassing the brittle, heuristic layout parsers and line segmenters that plague traditional pipelines. Recent empirical studies provide compelling, cross-domain evidence that VLMs substantially outperform traditional OCR systems on highly degraded historical documents, fundamentally altering the economic and technical landscape of mass archive digitization.

In a highly rigorous evaluation of 18th-century Russian Civil font texts—a domain characterized by complex historical orthography, non-standard ligatures, and highly degraded print quality that serves as an excellent technical analogue to 19th-century Swedish newsprint—frontier Vision LLMs demonstrated dramatic, order-of-magnitude superiority over traditional OCR systems. In this benchmark, the traditional Surya (BT5) model yielded a staggering 45.96% CER, and Tesseract 4.0 recorded a 21.55% CER paired with an abysmal 126.10% WER.10 Even PyLaia, a specialized historical OCR engine utilized via the Transkribus platform, managed only a 26.93% CER.10

In stark contrast, multimodal LLMs achieved transcription quality bordering on professional human-level precision without requiring any domain-specific fine-tuning. Google's Gemini 2.5 Pro achieved an industry-leading full-page CER of 3.36% and a WER of 4.69%, while its highly efficient counterpart, Gemini 2.5 Flash, recorded a highly respectable 4.94% CER.10 Other frontier models mirrored this success, with Qwen-2.5-VL achieving a 5.81% CER and OpenAI's GPT-4o reaching a 9.23% CER.10

Parallel research analyzing the extraction of text from the Nineteenth-Century Serials Edition (NCSE)—a massive archive containing 84,000 pages of highly degraded, text-dense English newspapers—corroborates these findings. Traditional OCR via Tesseract v5 failed significantly on these complex layouts, yielding an 18.5% CER primarily due to tight multi-column structures, vanishingly small financial fonts, and severe paper degradation.12 Conversely, Vision LLMs achieved exceptional historical fidelity. Claude 3.5 Sonnet recorded a 2.1% CER, and GPT-4o achieved a 2.4% CER.12 Notably, the open-weight Pixtral 12B model attained a highly competitive 3.2% mean CER, with an astonishing median CER of 1.0%, indicating that the vast majority of the documents were transcribed with near-perfect accuracy.12

The fundamental success of Vision LLMs in these environments lies in their capacity for deep contextual visual grounding. Unlike traditional CNN-LSTMs that predict individual characters based on narrow, isolated sliding windows of pixels, VLMs leverage billions of parameters of linguistic, semantic, and structural knowledge to probabilistically deduce ambiguous visual inputs. When a physical smudge obscures a numeric digit in a historical economic table, or ink bleed-through from the reverse page renders a dense Fraktur word optically illegible, the VLM utilizes the surrounding syntactic context, the semantic meaning of the table headers, and document-level patterns to predict the correct grapheme with high confidence.

The economics of scale heavily dictate pipeline architecture when processing years of daily economic publications. Processing massive batch jobs that would typically take days on rate-limited commercial OCR APIs can finish in hours on self-hosted open-source clusters, completely free of arbitrary API quotas.16 For truly massive historical archives, the usage costs of proprietary frontier models like OpenAI's GPT-4o or the o3 reasoning models can rapidly become prohibitive. In the evaluation of Russian historical texts, the OpenAI o3 model was explicitly excluded from the final study specifically due to "prohibitive usage costs".10

However, Google's Gemini 2.5 Flash offers a highly optimized middle ground for enterprise-scale extraction. It delivers a 15x cost reduction compared to its Pro counterpart—costing merely $0.15 per million input tokens and $0.60 per million output tokens—while maintaining a remarkably low CER and demonstrating blistering output speeds of approximately 256 tokens per second with sub-second first-token latency.14 Alternatively, the open-weight Pixtral 12B model represents a breakthrough for low-resource mass digitization initiatives. By downloading the model weights and running inference on consumer-grade hardware (such as dual RTX 4090 GPUs or a single A100), the effective inference cost drops to an estimated $0.002 per page, completely untethering the digitization effort from cloud subscription models.12

Beyond pure economics, stability and determinism fundamentally differentiate the architectures. Traditional CNN-LSTM pipelines are strictly deterministic; given the exact same physical image tensor, they will yield the exact same character sequence every single time. Vision LLMs, governed by stochastic generative processes and temperature settings, exhibit variable stability. Over multi-day stability testing on historical documents, Gemini 2.5 Pro demonstrated the highest consistency and lowest coefficient of variation (CV) of daily word accuracy, meaning its outputs rarely deviated.11 Conversely, models like Claude 3.5 exhibited significant daily variability, representing a major risk factor when building reproducible data pipelines.11

## **Systemic Biases, Failure Modes, and Epistemic Anachronisms in Vision LLMs**

The very mechanisms that grant Vision LLMs their superior zero-shot capabilities—their reliance on vast pre-training corpora, deep semantic priors, and global attention mechanisms—also introduce unique, pervasive failure modes that are entirely absent in traditional optical character recognition systems. When these advanced models are applied to non-standard historical typography and complex mixed scripts, they exhibit generative phenomena that, if left unchecked, can actively and silently corrupt the historical record. Understanding these biases is paramount before feeding VLM outputs into a strict grounding engine like langextract.

### **The Phenomenon of "Over-Historicization"**

The most striking and problematic systematic bias observed in multimodal LLMs when processing archival documents is the phenomenon termed "over-historicization." When processing historical materials, VLMs frequently hallucinate and insert archaic characters from incorrect, much older historical periods that do not actually exist in the physical source image.10 In the comprehensive evaluation of 18th-century Russian texts, LLMs systematically hallucinated obsolete Medieval Slavonic characters (such as ’ ѧ ’, ’ѡ ’, ’ ꙋ ’, and ’ ѿ ’) that had already been entirely eliminated by Peter the Great’s orthographic reforms decades prior to the printing of the documents.10

This phenomenon stems from a profound lack of "historical linguistic competence" within the models' latent space architectures. LLMs do not inherently model the continuum of typographical history with granular, decade-by-decade chronological boundaries. Instead, their training regimens force them to cluster rare, visually distinctive archaic forms into a broad, undifferentiated semantic space simply labeled "old" or "historical".11 When a vision encoder's attention head detects the visual signature of aged, yellowed paper, or identifies the non-standard geometric angles of a Fraktur script, it immediately activates probability distributions for a broad array of archaic characters. Consequently, the model hallucinates anachronistic glyphs, creating a temporal chimera that appears historically authentic to a modern layperson but is factually and optically inaccurate.19 This is a severe, systemic issue; GPT-4o, for instance, demonstrated this exact temporal bias by spontaneously inserting archaic characters into fully 59% of the evaluated historical files.10

### **The Modernization Trap and Silent Script Normalization**

Conversely, Vision LLMs also frequently fall victim to the exact opposite error: the "modernization trap." This occurs when the model automatically, and silently, "corrects" historical orthography to align with contemporary modern spelling standards.19 This failure mode is particularly prevalent in documents featuring mixed scripts or deprecated typographical conventions, such as the "long s" (ſ) that was ubiquitous in early 19th-century typography. Because the overwhelming, vast majority of an LLM's multi-trillion token pre-training data consists of modern, normalized internet text, the model possesses an incredibly strong statistical prior to output the modern lowercase "s" rather than the correct Unicode character for the long s (U+017F), even when the visual evidence in the image crop overwhelmingly points to the historical glyph.20

When processing Swedish economic newspapers from the critical 1880–1926 transition period, this modernization bias presents a massive hurdle. The text in these documents frequently shifts rapidly between dense, angular Fraktur for domestic reporting and rounded Antiqua for foreign loan words, scientific terms, or financial tickers.3 Vision LLMs can handle the semantic transition effortlessly, understanding the context of both scripts. However, they struggle immensely with transcription fidelity during these transitions. The models will often silently normalize older Swedish spellings (for example, standardizing archaic plural verb forms, or normalizing "q" to "k" or "c" depending on the exact decade of the publication) because the statistical sequence probability of the modern Swedish word drastically outweighs the raw optical evidence processed by the image encoder. The result is a text that reads perfectly in modern Swedish but is a flawed representation of the historical document.

### **Spatial Hallucinations in Tabular and Economic Data**

Extracting structured economic data—such as commodity prices, shipping manifests, and stock valuations—introduces an entirely separate vector of vulnerability to VLM hallucination. While modern VLMs have substantially mitigated their general tendency to hallucinate text, dense financial and tabular data remain critical, systemic failure points.

When parsing dense columns of numbers without clear demarcating lines or grid borders—a hallmark of 19th-century newsprint designed to maximize ink and paper efficiency—VLMs can easily lose their spatial grounding. In full-page processing modes, if an economic table features a large optical gap, a smudge, or a misaligned column, the VLM's generative nature compels it to "complete the pattern." Models have been observed inventing highly plausible numbers to fill perceived optical gaps, misaligning rows and columns to match a hallucinated schema, or even catching onto a repeating pattern of digits and replicating it indefinitely.21 For instance, despite generally high accuracy across broad text, targeted tests on structured historical generation have shown that models can occasionally completely fabricate dates and governmental structures to satisfy the semantic requirements of a prompt, even when provided with strict contextual grounding.23

Furthermore, traditional mitigation strategies for these errors actively make the problem worse. In traditional pipelines, post-OCR correction via LLMs is standard practice. However, empirical testing conclusively demonstrates that utilizing LLMs for post-OCR correction on historical documents actually degrades rather than improves overall performance. When a VLM is provided with both the raw image and a flawed baseline OCR text and instructed to correct it, higher-performing models often ignore the provided text entirely and re-decode the entire image from scratch, reintroducing their native temporal biases.10 Alternatively, text-only post-correction (without the guiding image) consistently introduces new, semantically plausible but factually incorrect errors, modernizing old words or guessing the wrong economic figure, thereby corrupting the original transcription beyond recovery.10 Therefore, the transcription layer must achieve maximum fidelity on the very first zero-shot pass.

## **The Architectural Imperative of Source Grounding in langextract**

The specific, immovable requirement to utilize Google's langextract library for the final structured extraction fundamentally dictates the technical boundaries and allowable error tolerances of the upstream OCR transcription layer. To architect this system correctly, one must understand exactly how langextract operates.

langextract is an advanced, open-source Python library engineered by Google to transform massive volumes of unstructured textual narratives into rigorous, schema-driven JSON data.25 It avoids the pitfalls of standard LLM wrappers by guiding an underlying language model (such as Gemini 2.5 Flash or Pro) through highly optimized text chunking algorithms, parallel batch processing, and few-shot controlled generation.27

However, the defining architectural feature of langextract—the feature that separates it from standard generative extraction—is its absolute reliance on "precise source grounding." Every extracted entity, relationship, or numerical data point output by the library is meticulously and mathematically mapped back to its exact character offset (start and end indices) within the original source document string.28 This precise mapping enables the automated generation of interactive HTML visualizations where human reviewers or auditors can click an extracted JSON node and see the exact word or number highlighted in the raw text, ensuring 100% traceability, auditability, and factual verifiability.26

Because langextract operates entirely as a text-to-text semantic engine, it operates under the strict assumption that the upstream text input it receives is a perfect, flawless reflection of the physical document. If the upstream OCR layer utilizes a Vision LLM that falls into the modernization trap—for example, silently normalizing a 10-character historical Swedish spelling into an 8-character modern spelling—the absolute character count of the document string shifts by \-2. If the VLM over-historicizes a passage by injecting non-existent Unicode symbols, the character count shifts positively.

When langextract subsequently processes this altered text and maps an extracted economic figure to character indices 4,502 through 4,508, the interactive visual highlighter will land on the completely wrong spatial location in the HTML interface because the underlying text string has been silently manipulated and resized by the generative OCR. The offsets are permanently broken. Furthermore, if the upstream VLM hallucinated a financial figure during the image-to-text transcription phase to satisfy a perceived spatial gap in a table, langextract will dutifully and flawlessly extract that hallucinated number as verified data, falsely grounding it in the corrupted text.

The pipeline metaphor used by the developers of these tools is highly apt: think of the upstream OCR engine as a meticulous librarian organizing loose pages into a bound book, and langextract as the expert researcher reading that book with a neon highlighter.31 If the librarian (the VLM) introduces subtle alterations, modernizations, or hallucinations into the bound text, the downstream researcher (langextract) will flawlessly highlight and catalog corrupted facts, presenting them as ground truth.31 Therefore, for a pipeline utilizing langextract, the upstream OCR transcription layer must ruthlessly prioritize exact, character-level fidelity over semantic fluency or modern readability.

## **Synthesized Pipeline Recommendation**

Given the absolute imperative for minimal hallucinations, high character-level fidelity, and the complex nature of highly degraded, mixed Fraktur/Antiqua Swedish economic newspapers, a simple off-the-shelf deployment of either architecture will fail. Traditional models like Tesseract and Kraken are deterministic and preserve character offsets, but their zero-shot baseline error rates on degraded 19th-century mixed scripts (ranging from 6% to 26% CER) are far too high to support automated economic data extraction, and fine-tuning them requires immense, cost-prohibitive investments in manually annotated ground truth.

Conversely, while Vision LLMs natively solve the mixed-script layout problem and provide vastly superior CERs out of the box, their inherent propensity for temporal bias, over-historicization, and silent spelling normalization directly threatens the integrity of langextract's required character-offset grounding mechanisms. To resolve this tension, the transcription layer must utilize a frontier VLM, but it must be rigidly bound by strict deterministic layout constraints and highly specific prompt engineering.

The recommended architectural pipeline for extracting structured economic data from the 1880–1926 Swedish archives is as follows:

### **Phase 1: Upstream Layout Parsing via Document Intelligence**

Do not feed the entire, unstructured historical newspaper broadsheet into the Vision LLM as a single image. Processing massive, multi-column pages maximizes the model's cognitive load and exponentially increases the probability of spatial hallucination and tabular misalignment.

Instead, the pipeline must utilize a deterministic upstream spatial parser. Integrating an open-source document intelligence toolkit like IBM's Docling allows the pipeline to perform rigorous layout analysis first.25 Docling utilizes specialized computer vision models—such as DocLayNet for layout analysis and TableFormer for table structure recognition—to dissect the complex newspaper page.25 It deterministically identifies headers, narrative text columns, and, most importantly, the complex boundaries of the economic tables.

Docling should be configured to isolate these financial tables and narrative columns, exporting them as distinct, high-resolution image crops. By dissecting the page prior to text generation, the system ensures that the downstream VLM is only processing small, structurally coherent crops, preventing it from losing spatial grounding and inventing numbers to fill complex broadsheet voids.

### **Phase 2: Zero-Shot Transcription via Gemini 2.5 Flash**

The high-resolution image crops generated by Docling should be passed individually to a Vision LLM for zero-shot text transcription. For this specific task, Google's Gemini 2.5 Flash is the recommended primary transcription engine.

While the open-weight Pixtral 12B offers a compelling local alternative with extreme cost efficiency, and Gemini 2.5 Pro offers slightly lower zero-shot CERs, Gemini 2.5 Flash provides the optimal, production-ready balance of intelligence, speed, and economic viability required for processing hundreds of thousands of complex newspaper pages. It achieves a highly competitive \~4.9% zero-shot CER on historical documents, processes at speeds of up to 256 tokens per second, and costs a fraction of the Pro model ($0.15 per 1M input tokens).10 Furthermore, keeping the transcription layer within the Gemini ecosystem ensures seamless integration and API continuity with the downstream langextract framework, which is natively optimized for Gemini models.30

If the processing budget permits, the pipeline should implement a dynamic routing mechanism. Standard narrative columns can be processed by Gemini 2.5 Flash, while highly degraded, extremely dense economic tables that fail initial heuristic validation passes should be routed to Gemini 2.5 Pro. The Pro model exhibits the highest stability (the lowest coefficient of variation over time) and superior visual grounding in complex spatial reasoning tasks, making it ideal for the most difficult tabular extractions.11

### **Phase 3: Anti-Normalization Prompt Engineering**

To neutralize the VLM's systemic biases of over-historicization and the modernization trap, Gemini 2.5 Flash must be deployed using highly engineered, context-enhanced system prompts. The prompt must not simply ask the model to "transcribe the image."

Instead, the system prompt must explicitly mandate a "diplomatic transcription"—an academic standard requiring the exact character-for-character replication of the source text, including original typographical errors and archaic spellings. The prompt must explicitly forbid the modernization of archaic Swedish spellings, the resolution of Fraktur ligatures into modern characters, and the generation of assumed text.

Crucially, to combat over-historicization, the prompt should provide the model with a bounded typographical context. It should list the specific Unicode values for expected archaic characters from the 1880–1926 period (such as the long s, U+017F), while explicitly banning the insertion of older medieval or Slavonic glyphs not native to the era.19 Empirical research confirms that providing VLMs with context-enhanced prompts detailing the specific historical orthography significantly reduces the Archaic Insertion Rate (AIR) and improves the Historical Character Preservation Rate (HCPR).10

### **Phase 4: Strict Avoidance of LLM Post-Correction**

The pipeline must strictly omit any secondary LLM-based post-OCR correction layers. While tempting as a final cleanup step, empirical testing on historical documents conclusively demonstrates that passing VLM-generated transcriptions back through a language model for text-only correction degrades historical fidelity. It introduces new semantic hallucinations, modernizes correct historical spellings, and fundamentally destroys the character offsets required for downstream grounding.10 The output from the prompted Gemini 2.5 Flash model must be treated as final.

### **Phase 5: Semantic Extraction and Grounding via langextract**

The high-fidelity, highly constrained raw text generated by the Vision LLM is then compiled and ingested by langextract. Because the VLM was restricted to diplomatic transcription and was fed clean layout crops by Docling, the character counts perfectly match the visual representation of the text.

The user can then define specific JSON schemas within langextract to identify economic entities—such as "Commodity," "Price," "Currency," and "Date." Guided by a few-shot example prompt, langextract will process the historical text, extract the precise economic data, and confidently map each data point to its exact character offset. The final output is a rigorously structured, highly verifiable dataset complete with HTML visualizations that allow economic historians to audit every single extracted number against its exact location in the original 19th-century Swedish archive.
